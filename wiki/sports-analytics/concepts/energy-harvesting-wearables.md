## Definition

Energy harvesting wearables are sensor devices that extract electrical power directly from the environment or the wearer's body—via mechanical motion, temperature gradients, light, or vibration—rather than relying on pre-charged batteries. This eliminates battery replacement/charging as a maintenance bottleneck and enables indefinitely long sensor operation.

## Origins

Energy harvesting research emerged in the 1990s-2000s as wearable and IoT systems created demand for maintenance-free sensors. Piezoelectric and electromagnetic energy harvesting were early technical approaches. Sports and health monitoring were identified as high-value application domains where continuous long-duration monitoring could significantly improve coaching/clinical insights.

## Key variants / extensions

- **Piezoelectric harvesting**: mechanical strain → electrical voltage (used in this sports-analytics track)
- **Electromagnetic induction**: motion → magnetic field change → induced current
- **Thermoelectric harvesting**: body temperature gradient → electrical voltage
- **Photovoltaic harvesting**: ambient light → electrical current (typically low-power)
- **Hybrid harvesters**: multiple energy sources for redundancy and reliability
