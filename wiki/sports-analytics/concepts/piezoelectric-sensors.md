## Definition

Piezoelectric sensors detect mechanical deformation (strain, stress, pressure, vibration, motion) by exploiting the piezoelectric effect: when certain crystalline materials are mechanically deformed, they generate an electrical voltage proportional to the deformation magnitude. The sensor requires no external power—mechanical energy is directly converted to electrical energy.

## Origins

The piezoelectric effect was discovered in 1880 by the Curie brothers in quartz crystals. Practical piezoelectric sensors emerged in the 20th century using natural quartz and synthetic ceramics like PZT (lead zirconate titanate). In recent decades, piezoelectric polymers (especially PVDF) have enabled flexible, conformal sensors for wearable applications.

## Key variants / extensions

- **Ceramic piezoelectric sensors**: PZT, AlN—high sensitivity, rigid, industrial standard
- **Piezoelectric polymers**: PVDF, PVDF-copolymers—flexible, conformal, lower cost, wearable-friendly
- **Multi-element arrays**: stacked or 2D arrays of piezoelectric elements for distributed sensing
- **Resonant piezoelectric sensors**: tuned to specific frequencies for enhanced sensitivity at target vibration frequencies
- **Bimorph and cantilever designs**: piezoelectric films mounted on elastic substrates to amplify strain
